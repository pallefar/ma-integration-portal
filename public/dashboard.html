Culture & Ways of Working

Culture integration is an employee-experience journey. People join a culture by finding answers to the questions that matter — guide them through each stage, then hand them the Ways-of-Working scenario cards to navigate real situations at TE.

1 What's Happening?

  • What happened, and why were we acquired?
  • Why now — and what will change?
  • What stays the same?
Enablers: CEO & Business narrative

2 How Does This Work?

  • Why so many stakeholders?
  • Why are decisions made differently?
  • Why does work feel more complex — and who decides?
Enablers: Ways-of-Working & Culture Translation cards

3 Do I Belong?

  • Do I fit here, and will my voice be heard?
  • Does my experience still matter?
  • Who can help me navigate?
Enablers: Buddy / Ambassador / ERG · 1:1 check-ins

4 How Can I Contribute?

  • How can I create value and make an impact?
  • How do we win together?
  • What can TE learn from us?
Enablers: Quick wins · Recognition · Growth & development
Employee journey: Understand Why → Understand How → Feel I Belong → Stay, Own & Contribute

Working in a Matrix Organization

Balancing project and functional priorities

Scenario

You lead a product launch that needs three engineers from Operations. Their functional manager has just moved two of them onto a factory upgrade with a hard deadline. Your sponsor still expects launch on the original date. Both leaders are right from their own side — and you have no direct authority over the engineers. Everyone is now looking to you to solve it.

Reflect
  • Who really owns this decision?
  • What trade-offs can I accept?
  • How do I raise this early, not late?
  • Who needs to be in the room?
What may feel different
  • You may answer to more than one leader.
  • Priorities shift fast and can compete.
  • You influence people you don't manage.
  • "Who decides?" isn't always clear.
How TE works
  • Align early on goals & trade-offs.
  • Escalate with a proposed solution.
  • Communicate openly across functions.
  • Decide together, then commit as one.
Do
  • Clarify roles & decision rights
  • Bring options and data
  • Flag conflicts early
  • Keep stakeholders informed
Don't
  • Wait and hope it resolves
  • Work around or go silent
  • Escalate with no fix proposed
  • Blame the other function
Who to ask
Project / Business Leader — scope & priorities
Functional Manager — resources & capacity
Key Stakeholders — impact & dependencies
Manager / Coach — approach & guidance
HRBP — role & tension support
Remember — in a matrix, influence beats authority. Align early, escalate smart, and decide as one team.

Integration Leader Academy

A guided course for Integration Leaders — the three integration layers, how to train the new organization, and how to grow into a strong Integration Leader. Work through each module and mark it complete to track your progress.

0 of 5 modules complete
1Communication Layer — how information flowsLayer▾

Every decision lands — and every site is heard. The playbook already pushes decisions down; the gap is the line back up. Add one trusted source, the "why" behind each call, and a real return path.

Four moves that close the comms control gap:

  1. Own one source of truth — one owner, one place for every decision and answer, so no site runs on rumor.
  2. Explain every decision — what was decided, why, and what's next, on a set cadence.
  3. Open the line back up — sites send questions, concerns and local strengths up early, so they shape decisions.
  4. Close every loop — answer every question and show how input shaped the call.

Track it: Adoption → Time-to-Answer → Loops Closed → Retention.

2Culture Layer — how employees experience TELayer▾

Culture integration is an employee-experience journey. Employees join a culture by finding answers to the questions that matter, in order:

  1. What's Happening? — what changed and why.
  2. How Does This Work? — stakeholders, decisions, the operating model.
  3. Do I Belong? — fit, voice, and who helps me navigate.
  4. How Can I Contribute? — value, impact, and what TE can learn from them.

Enablers: CEO/business narrative · Ways-of-Working & Culture Translation cards · Buddy/Ambassador/ERG · recognition & growth. See the Culture & Ways of Working tab for the full toolkit.

3Onboarding & Capability — how people and HR succeedLayer▾

Enable successful people integration across the first 100 days and beyond in four stages:

  1. Assess — capability assessment, leadership review, skills mapping, critical-talent identification.
  2. Onboard — integration roadmap, HR & manager onboarding packs, role clarity, 30/60/90-day guidance.
  3. Connect — mentor pairing, site visits, meet-the-team, functional champions.
  4. Develop — development plans, TE ways of working, learning resources; for TE HR: capability building, AI guidance, manager coaching, lessons learned.
4How to Train the New OrganizationPractice▾

Turn the playbook into practice for the acquired organization:

  • Run Ways-of-Working scenario cards in team settings — start with the Matrix Organization card.
  • Stand up the Buddy Plant Network for two-sided learning between the acquired site and TE.
  • Give every new hire a 30/60/90-day plan with explicit role clarity.
  • Use manager onboarding packs so leaders cascade a consistent message.
  • Localize, don't impose — lead with local HR leaders and translate TE norms into the site's context.
  • Measure adoption and close every loop.
5Becoming a Strong Integration LeaderGrow▾

You lead across a matrix where you have influence, not authority. Strong Integration Leaders:

  • Align early on goals and trade-offs, before positions harden.
  • Escalate smart — bring a proposed solution, options and data; flag conflicts early.
  • Communicate openly across functions — decide together, then commit as one team.
  • Coach the acquired org through the culture journey; make every employee feel heard.
  • Close every loop and show how input shaped decisions — that is how trust compounds.
Remember — in a matrix, influence beats authority. Align early, escalate smart, and decide as one team.
